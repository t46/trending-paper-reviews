Paper overview

SEATS targets the prefill bottleneck in omni-modal LLMs, where temporally aligned visual and audio tokens are expensive to carry through every language-model layer. The paper's central observation is depth-dependent: non-textual token dependence appears strongest in shallow layers, weaker in middle layers, and negligible in late layers.

The method maps that observation into a training-free stage-adaptive token selection strategy. It combines pre-LLM diversity selection, inner-LLM query-guided budget allocation, and late-layer removal of non-textual tokens, with separate handling for audio/video streams and stage-specific retention schedules.

Experiments on Qwen2.5-Omni and Qwen3-Omni cover five audio-video benchmarks, multiple retention ratios, efficiency metrics, and ablations. The evidence supports SEATS as a strong practical acceleration method, while the paper's most consequential open questions concern public reproducibility, uncertainty around close accuracy differences, and validation of the stage boundaries beyond the diagnostic benchmark used to motivate them.

Key strengths

Coherent stage-adaptive design. The paper does more than prune tokens uniformly: it links a layer-wise audio/video dependence pattern to a shallow-middle-late schedule, making the method conceptually cleaner than a single global token budget.

Strong efficiency evidence across two backbones. The reported FLOPs reductions and prefill speedups are substantial on both Qwen2.5-Omni and Qwen3-Omni, while most full-token task performance is preserved under aggressive retention settings.

Relevant training-free comparison set. The baseline suite includes omni-modal token selection methods as well as adapted image and video pruning methods such as OmniZip, FastV, VisionZip, and DivPrune, which is the right neighborhood for evaluating a training-free inference method.

Useful component ablations. The ablation study separates the effects of inner-LLM selection, decay scheduling, cross-modal allocation, and late-layer removal, giving readers a plausible account of which pieces drive the observed efficiency-performance tradeoff.

Detailed paper-level reproduction notes. Appendix material documents retention ratios, baseline adaptation choices, and official repositories. Those notes do not replace executable code, but they make the intended experimental setup clearer than the main tables alone would.

Key concerns and constructive suggestions

The public repository currently has no implementation. The paper and metadata point to a GitHub repository, but the available repository does not contain SEATS source code, dependency files, evaluation scripts, or baseline implementations. To address this, release a tagged reproduction package with SEATS modules, Qwen integration patches, LMMs-Eval adapters, exact configs, dependency lockfiles, and scripts for the main tables.

Baseline adaptation fairness cannot yet be audited. Several comparison methods are adapted outside their original target modality, and Appendix B.2 describes these adaptations only at a narrative level. To address this, publish each adapted baseline as a separate config or wrapper with source commit, modified files, retained-token accounting, and validation against the original method's expected behavior.

Close accuracy gaps need uncertainty reporting. Some reported differences against the closest baselines are small enough that run-level variance, bootstrap confidence intervals, or paired tests could change how the rankings are interpreted. To address this, add confidence intervals over evaluation instances, repeated-run intervals where seeds are involved, and paired tests whenever per-example outcomes are available.

The layer-dependency motivation is narrower than the evaluation scope. The stage schedule is motivated by WorldSense-derived layer-removal diagnostics, but the method is then applied across Daily-Omni, OmniVideoBench, Video-MME, and LVOmniBench as well. To address this, add Figure-2-style diagnostics for each benchmark or show through transfer ablations that the chosen boundaries do not cause measurable loss outside the motivating benchmark.

Manual hyperparameters limit deployment confidence. Transition layers, decay parameters, allocation thresholds, and per-backbone ratios are manually selected, which makes it harder to know how much calibration is needed for a new omni-modal backbone. To address this, provide a low-cost boundary and hyperparameter calibration recipe, then report sensitivity around the selected values and include a concrete streaming-inference stress test.

What would most improve this paper

1. Release the complete code and reproduction package. This is the highest-leverage change: it would make the SEATS implementation, baseline adaptations, and paper-code consistency independently inspectable.

2. Add statistical uncertainty to the main results. Confidence intervals and paired tests would distinguish robust efficiency-performance conclusions from close point-estimate rankings.

3. Validate baseline adaptations explicitly. Baseline wrappers with source commits, configs, and behavior checks would make the comparison set substantially more credible.

4. Extend layer-dependency validation across benchmarks. Per-benchmark diagnostics would show whether the shallow/middle/late schedule is a general omni-modal pattern or a useful heuristic tuned to the motivating analysis.

5. Reframe scope and deployment guidance. Position the contribution precisely as training-free omni-modal inference acceleration, clarify its relation to trainable methods such as OmniSIFT and EchoingPixels, and add a checklist for adapting SEATS to new om-LLMs.
