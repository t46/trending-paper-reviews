Paper overview

Next-Acceleration-Scale Prediction for Autoregressive MRI Reconstruction tackles the ill-posedness of accelerated MRI reconstruction, especially under extreme undersampling where many anatomically plausible images can match incomplete measurements. The paper argues that continuous pixel-domain predictors tend to average over feasible solutions and lose high-frequency anatomy, motivating a discrete latent-space formulation.

The method represents reconstructions with an additive multi-input vector-quantized autoencoder and trains a cross-attentive transformer to predict the next acceleration scale autoregressively. A privileged teacher with access to fully sampled acquisitions supervises student rollouts through on-policy distillation, aiming to reduce rollout mismatch while preserving detail.

Experiments focus on fastMRI brain reconstruction at R=32 across several mask patterns and contrasts. The paper is most persuasive as a research prototype for perceptual reconstruction under difficult Cartesian undersampling, while its case for clinical reliability and benchmark reproducibility remains incomplete.

Key strengths

Creative problem reformulation. The acceleration-scale prediction setup adapts visual autoregressive modeling to MRI in a domain-aware way, rather than simply applying a generic image generator to reconstructed images.

Well-aligned components. The AQ-VAE tokenizer, cross-attention mechanism, and on-policy privileged distillation each address a specific reconstruction failure mode: representation constraints, spatial fidelity, and train-test rollout mismatch.

Useful empirical coverage within the chosen setting. The R=32 fastMRI experiments compare against a broad suite of UNet, transformer, unrolled, physics-informed, diffusion, and Mamba-style baselines, and the ablations probe the main architectural and distillation choices.

Honest limitation framing. The paper acknowledges that R=32 should not be interpreted as routine clinical readiness and notes that discrete tokenization can cap fidelity, which helps keep the technical claims more grounded.

Key concerns and constructive suggestions

Reproducibility is currently blocked. The linked repository exists, but it does not contain the implementation needed to audit the tokenizer, transformer training, distillation procedure, baselines, or evaluation scripts. To address this, the authors should release runnable code with environment files, data preparation instructions, model configs, checkpoints or training recipes, and exact evaluation commands.

Mean-only result tables understate uncertainty. The main comparisons are reported as point estimates, which makes it hard to judge whether close differences across masks, contrasts, and baselines are robust. To address this, the authors should add confidence intervals, paired tests, per-slice or per-subject distributions, and stratified failure-case plots for the headline results.

Clinical fidelity remains proxy-based. The evidence emphasizes perceptual and pixel metrics, but sharper images in a generative medical reconstruction setting may not correspond to diagnostically faithful anatomy. To address this, the authors should add reader evaluation, a downstream diagnostic or segmentation proxy, and explicit separation between perceptual quality, pixel fidelity, measurement consistency, and clinical usefulness.

Hallucination and data-consistency risk need direct testing. A learned discrete prior can plausibly restore texture while inventing structures not sufficiently supported by measured k-space. To address this, the authors should report k-space residuals, data-consistency checks, uncertainty maps, error maps, and targeted analyses of false plausible structures under weak measurements.

Generalization is narrow. The evaluation concentrates on fastMRI brain data at R=32, leaving unclear whether the gains persist at common acceleration factors, across anatomy, scanner/site shifts, or prospective-style masks. To address this, the authors should add R=4/8/12/16 alongside R=32 and include external brain, knee, scanner/site split, or prospectively motivated evaluations.

What would most improve this paper

1. Release a full reproducibility package. The fastest quality lift is a complete implementation with configs, environment, data preparation, training, evaluation, and baseline reproduction scripts.

2. Add statistical and per-case evidence. Confidence intervals, paired tests, effect sizes, and per-case distributions would clarify which improvements are reliable and where the model fails.

3. Audit measurement consistency and hallucination risk. k-space residuals, reconstruction error maps, uncertainty estimates, and false-structure analysis would make the perceptual gains safer to interpret.

4. Add clinical or task-relevant validation. A small radiologist reader study, diagnostic acceptability test, or downstream segmentation/lesion-detection proxy would connect visual sharpness to medical usefulness.

5. Broaden acceleration and domain coverage. Testing lower acceleration regimes, external sites, other anatomy, and prospective-style masks would show whether the method is a general reconstruction strategy or mainly an extreme-undersampling demonstration.
