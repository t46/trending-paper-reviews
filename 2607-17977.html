RynnBrain 1.1’s strongest contribution is a coherent, open embodied-model family that links spatial understanding, contact prediction, planning, and cross-embodiment control, with promising same-recipe robot comparisons. The most important next step is to isolate component effects and report uncertainty, functional contact outcomes, held-out transfer, and the missing implementation details needed for independent reproduction.

Paper overview

RynnBrain 1.1 presents a family of embodied foundation models at 2B, 9B, and 122B-A10B scales. It unifies spatio-temporal and physically grounded pretraining for perception, spatial reasoning, localization, and planning, extending the earlier line with native 3D grounding and contact-point prediction for smaller variants.

The report also introduces RynnBrain-VLA, a cross-embodiment policy using an 81-dimensional masked action space. The system is evaluated across cognition and localization benchmarks, 3D outputs, and real-robot tasks on Unitree G1, Astribot-S1, and Tianji-Wuji.

Its headline evidence combines broad benchmark coverage with same-recipe control comparisons: the report gives an average final success of 86.67% for RynnBrain-VLA versus 60.00% for the corresponding Qwen control. The release includes checkpoints, a project page, and code, but the public repository snapshot does not expose the complete training, VLA, evaluation, or robot-driver implementation.

Key strengths

Integrated embodied scope. One model family connects cognition, localization, 3D understanding, contact, planning, and action, making the package practically relevant and easy to inspect as a capability taxonomy.

Concrete physical outputs. Oriented 3D boxes and compact contact (p, θ) outputs are closer to manipulation than free-form language descriptions and give the system a useful interface to downstream control.

Broad evaluation surface. Tables 3–6 cover multiple benchmarks, three hardware platforms, and both base-model and VLA behavior, providing more evidence than a single-task demonstration.

Useful same-recipe control. The RynnBrain-VLA versus Qwen-based-VLA comparison holds the post-training recipe more nearly constant and reports consistent task-level gains, which is a valuable starting point for attribution.

Meaningful openness. Public checkpoints, a project page, and links to GitHub, Hugging Face, and ModelScope make external inspection and follow-up work possible.

Key concerns and constructive suggestions

Uncertainty is missing. Tables 3–6 primarily provide point estimates; the 20-trial protocol does not report confidence intervals, repeated training seeds, or significance tests. To address this, publish trial-level outcomes, binomial confidence intervals, seed-level benchmark results, and effect sizes before describing small gains as robust.

Central implementation details are not reproducible from the repository. The inspected public snapshot contains inference/demo materials but not the training, VLA, action-mask, evaluation, or robot implementation needed to recreate the central transfer claim. To address this, release pinned source, configs, manifests, prompts, seeds, evaluation scripts, robot calibration, and a containerized reproduction bundle.

Component attribution and generalization are under-tested. The base model, data mixture, 3D/contact supervision, architecture, and VLA recipe change together, while evaluations use selected adapted tasks rather than a clearly held-out transfer protocol. To address this, run matched factorial ablations and hold out embodiments, objects, scenes, instructions, and camera configurations for zero- or few-shot tests.

Contact prediction is evaluated qualitatively. Figure 7 examples show plausible contacts, but do not establish geometric accuracy or closed-loop usefulness. To address this, report pixel and angle error, top-k valid contacts, human or robot agreement, and downstream grasp success with failure cases.

Novelty positioning needs precision. Prior work already contains related 3D grounding and shared embodied/action representations, so the distinct claim is best located in RynnBrain’s particular integration and interface. To address this, add a component-lineage table and matched comparisons that identify the measurable delta over each relevant precedent.

Generated labels need reliability audits. Qwen-VL and Gemini-generated annotations introduce a potentially important source of label noise, but prompt and agreement details are limited. To address this, publish prompt versions, human agreement samples, error categories, and source-wise sensitivity experiments.

What would most improve this paper

1. Add uncertainty and trial-level evidence. Repeated seeds, raw 20-trial outcomes, confidence intervals, and pre-specified effect-size analyses would show which headline differences exceed sampling variation.

2. Release a full reproduction bundle. Versioned training/VLA/evaluation code, data manifests, prompts, configs, seeds, baseline adapters, and robot settings would turn the current openness into independently testable evidence.

3. Isolate the component contributions. Matched-data, matched-compute ablations for 3D supervision, contact representation, data sources, action masking, and joint training would clarify what drives the gains.

4. Test genuine transfer and functional contact. Held-out embodiments, objects, scenes, and viewpoints, paired with quantitative contact metrics and closed-loop grasp outcomes, would bound the generalization claims.

5. Tighten claims and provenance. A claim-to-evidence matrix, explicit result provenance, failure/latency/safety reporting, and a more precise related-work discussion would make the report easier to audit and build on.
