## Introduction Findings (Section 1)

### Problem Statement
> "action-labelled data is often scarce and therefore scaling-up foundation models for decision-making remains a challenge"
> — Abstract

AVID addresses adapting closed-source pretrained video diffusion models to world models using lightweight adapters on small domain-specific datasets.

### Claimed Contributions
1. "Proposing to adapt pretrained video diffusion models to action-conditioned world models, without access to the parameters of the pretrained model." (Abstract)
2. "Analyzing the limitations of the adaptation approach proposed by Yang et al." — proving naive composition produces biased samples (Section 3.2)
3. "AVID, a novel approach...applies a learned mask to the outputs of a pretrained model, and combines them with conditional outputs learned by a domain-specific adapter." (Abstract)

### Claimed Gaps in Prior Work
> "the most powerful models are closed-source which means they cannot be finetuned"
> — Introduction

## Method Section Findings (Section 3) — CRITICAL

### Overall Approach
AVID trains a lightweight 3D UNet adapter that takes as input: the noisy video, the pretrained model's noise prediction, and the initial image (repeated). The adapter outputs a mask m and its own noise prediction, which are combined via learned spatial masking to produce the final noise prediction.

### Key Technical Components

#### Component 1: Adapter Architecture (Section 3.3)
> "a 3D UNet consisting of a sequence of spatio-temporal blocks with residual connections"
> — Section 3.3

> "Input structure: The UNet accepts a tensor of shape R^(T×h×w×3c) comprising three concatenated components: (1) the noisy video x_i, (2) the output of the pretrained model epsilon_pre(x_i,i,x_0), and (3) the initial image x^0 repeated T times across the time dimension."
> — Section 3.3

**Technical Details**:
- 3D convolution + spatial attention + temporal attention per spatio-temporal block
- Outputs tensor of shape R^(T×h×w×(c+1)): mask m plus noise prediction
- Pretrained model parameters remain completely frozen
- Only adapter parameters theta_adapt trained

#### Component 2: Masking Mechanism (Section 3.3, Equation 5)
> "epsilon_final(x_i, a, i, x_0) = epsilon_pre ⊙ m + epsilon_adapt ⊙ (1−m)"
> — Section 3.3, Eq. 5

where m ∈ [0,1] via sigmoid activation, ⊙ is element-wise multiplication.

**Technical Details**:
- Mask operates at full spatial-temporal resolution
- Allows spatially selective blending: some regions use pretrained predictions, others use adapter predictions
- Key design insight: avoids naive addition of noise predictions (proven biased in Section 3.2)

#### Component 3: Action Encoding (Section 3.3)
> "we embed the corresponding action a^T to compute the action embedding e_a^tau using an embedding table" (for discrete actions)
> "using a linear layer for continuous actions"
> — Section 3.3

> "These embeddings combine with diffusion step embeddings: concatenated and processed by an MLP to compute the scale and shift parameters, gamma^tau and beta^tau, for the tau-th frame"
> — Section 3.3

**Technical Details**:
- Per-frame scale/shift parameters derived from action and timestep embeddings
- Applied after each 3D convolution (adaptive normalization)
- Supports both discrete and continuous actions

#### Component 4: Training (Section 3.3, Equation 6)
> "L(theta_adapt) = E ||epsilon_final(x_i, a, i, x_0) − epsilon||^2"
> — Section 3.3, Eq. 6

**Technical Details**:
- Standard denoising loss
- Only adapter parameters optimized
- Pretrained model runs in inference mode (frozen)

#### Component 5: Theoretical Motivation (Section 3.2)
The paper proves that the prior "Product of Experts" approach (Yang et al.):
> "epsilon_PoE(x_i, i, c) := epsilon_pre(x_i, i, c) + epsilon_adapt(x_i, i, c)"
produces biased samples because "convolution does not distribute over multiplication."

**Technical Details**:
- Biased because true score function is not additive for independent models
- AVID's masked composition avoids this theoretical flaw

### Algorithm Summary
1. Forward pass: noisy video + initial frame → pretrained model → epsilon_pre
2. Adapter input: concatenate (noisy video, epsilon_pre, repeated initial frame)
3. Adapter forward pass → (mask m, epsilon_adapt)
4. Combine: epsilon_final = epsilon_pre ⊙ m + epsilon_adapt ⊙ (1-m)
5. Training loss: ||epsilon_final - epsilon_true||^2

### Problem Overlap Assessment
**Overlap Level**: High — same technical problem domain

**Comparison**: AVID and NC_GUIWorld both take a pretrained video diffusion model and add lightweight action conditioning modules. The application domains differ (game/robotics vs. OS/GUI), but the core technical question — "how to inject action conditioning into a pretrained video diffusion model" — is identical.

### Method Overlap Assessment
**Overlap Level**: Medium — same problem, different technical solution

**Evidence (AVID)**:
> "applies a learned mask to the outputs of a pretrained model, and combines them with conditional outputs learned by a domain-specific adapter"
> — Abstract

**Evidence (NC_GUIWorld from project blog)**:
NC_GUIWorld studies four injection modes: external (input-side), contextual (token merging), residual (side branch), and internal (cross-attention inside blocks). The "residual" mode is described as "ControlNet-style."

**Comparison**:
- AVID works without access to model weights (adapter modifies outputs only)
- NC_GUIWorld requires access to model weights (fine-tuning the base model)
- AVID uses output-space masking; NC_GUIWorld uses weight-space fine-tuning
- Both study multiple injection patterns for actions
- Both use per-frame adaptive normalization for action conditioning
- NC_GUIWorld's "external" and "residual" modes share conceptual overlap with AVID's approach

### Potential Novelty Conflict

**Specific evidence**:

AVID (October 2024, ICLR 2025) demonstrates that pretrained video diffusion models can be adapted to action-conditioned world models using lightweight adapters. NC_GUIWorld (April 2026) performs the same adaptation for OS/GUI environments with fine-tuning access to model weights.
