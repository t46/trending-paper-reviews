EMO: Pretraining Mixture of Experts for Emergent Modularity

Wang, Bhagia & Min (UC Berkeley, Ai2) · May 2026 · arXiv:2605.06663

EMO contributes two verified novel mechanisms — a document-boundary supervisory signal and dynamic-d sampling — and an unusually robust Pareto gap (Cohen’s d ≈ 7.2) at 25% expert retention against a matched standard MoE, paired with above-typical artifact release. The most consequential improvement direction is calibration of claims to evidence: the “1%/3% drop” headline bundles three sub-claims of unequal robustness on single-seed runs, the §2 ModuleFormer reproduction is incomplete in three independently-disqualifying ways, and a five-paper citation chain (Mod-Squad, MoCE, MoE Routing Testbed, EMoE, Sim-Pres) is missing — most of which is fixable in a framing pass without redoing the central science.

Paper overview

EMO targets a structural limitation of standard Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) language models: even though they activate only a subset of experts per token, restricting inference to a subset of experts for a given domain causes severe performance degradation, undermining their use in memory-constrained deployment. The paper introduces a pretraining method designed to make expert subsets independently usable and composable — that is, modular — without any human-defined priors.

The core mechanism is a per-document expert-pool constraint. Tokens within a single document are forced to select experts from a shared pool of size d, while different documents use different pools. Because tokens within a document tend to share a domain, this constraint encourages coherent expert groupings to emerge from document boundaries alone. The pool size d is itself sampled per document during training (dynamic-d), so the same model supports a range of subset sizes at inference.

Empirically, the authors pretrain a 1B-active, 14B-total EMO model on 1T tokens of OLMoE-mix-0824. They report:

  • Full-capacity parity: EMO is competitive with a matched standard MoE (“Reg.MoE”) at the full d = 128 setting.
  • Subset-d retention: at 25% of experts, EMO loses ~1pp absolute (vs. ~10pp for the standard MoE); at 12.5%, ~3pp.
  • Semantic specialization: expert subsets correspond to interpretable domains (e.g., math, code) rather than the low-level syntactic clusters typical of standard MoEs.

The artifact release is above-typical for the field: model weights on Hugging Face, code under Apache-2.0 on GitHub, a blog post, and an interactive visualization.

Key strengths

Two verified novel mechanisms. A deep cross-check against ten prior works — including ModuleFormer, Mod-Squad, MoCE, EMoE, and the MoE Routing Testbed — finds no preemption of (i) the document-boundary supervisory signal or (ii) the dynamic-d sampling scheme. Both are genuinely original contributions to MoE training-objective design.

A statistically robust Pareto gap at d = 32. The flagship comparison — EMO at 25% expert retention vs. a matched-architecture, matched-data, matched-tokens, matched-hyperparameter Reg.MoE at the same retention — yields an estimated Cohen’s d ≈ 7.2 on MMLU. The effect is robust under any plausible per-seed standard deviation up to 2pp, meaning this sub-claim survives even pessimistic noise assumptions.

An unusually clean matched-baseline construction. A code-level audit confirms that Reg.MoE shares architecture, data ordering, token budget, optimizer, and hyperparameters with EMO — a level of matching not always achieved in MoE re-implementation studies.

Above-typical artifact release. Apache-2.0 code, public matched-baseline weights, a Hugging Face model release, blog, and visualization together place the paper at the upper end of reproducibility for 1B-active MoE pretraining work.

Semantic specialization is a qualitatively striking finding. The shift from low-level syntactic clusters (typical of standard MoE) to interpretable semantic domains (math, code, etc.) is a real qualitative change with implications for compositional and modular deployment beyond the immediate paper.

Key concerns and constructive suggestions

The §2 ModuleFormer reproduction is incomplete in three independently-disqualifying ways. A code-level audit shows that mutualinfo_router.py implements only the pretrain MI loss; the finetune-concentration step from ModuleFormer §4.2 Eq. 8 — the very objective that produces ModuleFormer’s 30–75% retention regime — is absent. The reproduction launcher (scripts/models/modulemoe_*.sh) is not in the public repo despite the rest of the code being Apache-2.0, and no numerical row appears in any table. The “consistent with their reported results” framing therefore misrepresents what was reproduced. To address this, the authors should either (a) add a ModuleFormer numerical row at matched scale with the finetune-concentration step explicitly enabled (and release the launcher), or (b) drop the “we attempted to reproduce” clause and rest the contrast on the architectural distinction (one objective vs. two), which is defensible from ModuleFormer’s own §4.1–§4.2.

A five-paper citation chain is missing, including one property-preempting work. Mod-Squad (Z. Chen et al., CVPR 2023) demonstrated single-objective standalone-subset = full-model performance in vision multi-task at 42% retention — a verbatim preemption of EMO’s N2 property claim. ModuleFormer (which EMO does cite) itself cites Mod-Squad, and the two papers share a senior author. Four further adjacent works are also uncited: MoCE (structurally adjacent two-stage routing), MoE Routing Testbed (concurrent confirmation that balancing scope drives specialization at 0.8B/9.6B), EMoE (title-level “Emergent Modularity” terminology precedence), and Similarity-Preserving Routers + Task-Cond Routing Signatures. To address this, the authors should add all five citations in a single Related Work refresh and reframe N2 as “the same property in language pretraining without task labels” — this preserves a real novel contribution while preempting reviewer pushback.

The “1%/3% drop” headline bundles three sub-claims of unequal robustness. A sub-claim decomposition shows: (a) EMO vs. Reg.MoE at d = 32 is robustly attributed (d ≈ 7.2); (b) EMO vs. Reg.MoE at d = 128 has a signed mean Δ = −0.98pp with 4-of-5 negative deltas (the “matches” framing is directionally misleading); (c) EMO’s absolute drop on GSM8K is within plausible single-seed noise. A typical reader internalizes all three as fully supported, but only (a) is. To address this, the abstract and §5.1 should disambiguate the three sub-claims explicitly — e.g., “competitive at full capacity with small consistent losses on Gen5/GSM8K (~1.5–2pp); ~10pp Pareto gap at 25% experts vs. a matched standard MoE.”

Single-seed regime with no variance reporting on a 4×-repeated headline. All main results in Tables 1, 3 and Figures 3, 11 are single-seed. No confidence intervals, standard deviations, or significance tests appear anywhere in the headline tables, despite the “1%/3% drop” claim being asserted four times. Compute constraints at the 1T-token scale are real, but the paper already runs many ablations at 130B tokens where multi-seed is feasible. To address this, the authors should run 3–5 seeds at the 130B-token scale for Reg.MoE and EMO at d = 128 and d = 32 across all five evaluation suites, and report bootstrap-of-test-items 95% CIs (which add no extra training cost).

The specialization-emergence story at d = 128 is partially confounded by inherited Global LBL. Triangulating Global LBL (Qiu et al. 2025, Fig 1b at 3.4–43B) with the MoE Routing Testbed (§4.2.1, §4.2.5 at 0.8B/9.6B) suggests that balancing scope alone — which Reg.MoE inherits — drives much of what §5.3 attributes to EMO. EMO’s unique value-add is concentrated in the d < 128 regime. To address this, the authors should either run an ablation isolating per-document pool from global LBL (document-pool-only and global-LBL-only at d = 128), or reframe §5.3 to acknowledge inheritance: “EMO’s per-document constraint adds a structural mechanism on top of global LBL that improves subset-d performance; specialization-emergence at d = 128 is largely attributable to the inherited global LBL.”

Two appendix narratives contradict their own tables on per-axis disaggregation. §A.3 claims “incorporating shared experts improves performance in EMO,” but Table 2’s averaged effect across d is −0.16pp — shared experts help only at full k = 128 and hurt at every smaller k. §B.4 claims EMO improves over EMO-anneal “across most tasks and expert subset sizes,” but on GSM8K, 3-of-5 d-values favor EMO-anneal. To address this, both narratives should be revised with per-d / per-task disaggregation surfaced.

No Limitations section in a 25-page paper. Despite 11 main pages plus 14 appendix pages, there is no dedicated Limitations section. The two load-bearing assumptions — documents are domain-coherent (MA-1) and per-document expert pools transfer to task subsets (MA-2) — are unvalidated at corpus level even though both are runnable from the released code+data without new training. To address this, the authors should add a §7 Limitations section listing MA-1, MA-2, single-seed, single-corpus, English-only, the §5.3 attribution caveat, and the ModuleFormer reproduction caveat — and run the corpus diagnostics (WebOrganizer 24-domain classifier on 1K documents; per-document D overlap with MMLU-math task subsets) using the existing release.

What would most improve this paper

1. Citation-hygiene refresh (presentation-only). Add Mod-Squad, MoCE, MoE Routing Testbed, EMoE, and Sim-Pres + Task-Cond in a single coherent Related Work pass, and reframe N2 as “the same property in language pretraining without task labels.” This preempts reviewer pushback and strengthens rather than weakens the contribution. Low effort, high impact.

2. Multi-seed at the 130B-token scale (one major experiment). Run 3–5 seeds for Reg.MoE and EMO at d = 128 and d = 32 across all five evaluation suites; compute Cohen’s d per sub-claim; report bootstrap CIs. This single experiment resolves the entire statistical-rigor cluster (single-seed + bundled headline + “matches” framing).

3. ModuleFormer reproduction resolution. Either complete the reproduction with the finetune-concentration step and release the launcher (option a), or drop the “we attempted to reproduce” clause and rest the contrast on the architectural distinction (option b, presentation-only). Either path retires the only critical-severity finding.

4. Headline reframe + Limitations section (presentation-only). Disambiguate the three sub-claims of the “1%/3% drop” headline in the abstract and §5.1; replace “matches Reg.MoE” with “competitive at d = 128 with small consistent losses on Gen5/GSM8K.” Add a §7 Limitations section listing the load-bearing assumptions, single-seed regime, single-corpus / English-only scope, and the §5.3 attribution caveat.

5. MA-1 / MA-2 corpus diagnostics via the released artifacts (analysis-only). Run WebOrganizer’s 24-domain classifier on 1K OLMoE-mix-0824 documents to report the fraction of single-topic documents (MA-1); compute per-document D and validation-derived task-subset overlap for MMLU-math (MA-2). Both are runnable from the existing release without new training and would empirically ground — or constrain — the §5.3 narrative.
