Paper overview

MedPMC presents a five-stage framework for converting permissively licensed PubMed Central literature into training data for medical vision-language models. Applied to 6.1 million articles, the pipeline produces 11 million image-text pairs through medical relevance screening, multi-panel figure handling, figure separation, caption extraction and alignment, and medical figure classification.

The paper evaluates both the curation components and the downstream value of the resulting corpus. Manual review finds markedly higher medical relevance than a prior PMC-derived dataset, while a CLIP-style model trained on MedPMC improves average zero-shot performance across 26 benchmarks spanning 11 specialties. The same representation also improves medical visual question answering and morphology-to-image retrieval on 10,524 Yale New Haven Health System dermatology photographs.

This is best understood as an open-data and curation blueprint with promising transfer evidence, not as evidence of deployment readiness. The paper’s central unresolved question is whether independent researchers can reproduce the public-data results and separate genuine curation gains from benchmark overlap, selective filtering, and patient-level dependence.

Key strengths

A high-value bottleneck is addressed directly. The framework targets the scarcity of large, expert-authored clinical image-text data by making systematic use of PMC’s permissively licensed literature, creating a resource with clear practical value for medical multimodal research.

The pipeline is modular and unusually auditable in design. Its five stages isolate distinct failure modes, and held-out component evaluations provide useful evidence about screening, panel detection, figure separation, and caption alignment rather than treating curation as an opaque preprocessing step.

The downstream evaluation is broad and triangulated. Results span 26 benchmarks and 11 specialties, two medical VQA settings, and an out-of-domain dermatology retrieval probe. The architecture-matched corpus replacement is a particularly useful control because it keeps the model recipe fixed while testing the supervision source.

The paper is candid about important limits. It acknowledges possible benchmark overlap and correctly avoids presenting the Yale New Haven Health System retrieval experiment as deployment validation. That restraint makes the positive transfer evidence more credible.

Key concerns and constructive suggestions

The public release does not yet match the reproducibility claim. The inspected official repository contains only a README while the paper describes code, benchmarks, and training recipes as available. To address this, publish an immutable paper-version tag containing the exact code, dependencies, configurations, manifests, checkpoints, evaluators, and an end-to-end smoke test; otherwise revise the availability wording to describe what is actually released.

Benchmark contamination is not demonstrated away. The paper excludes some curated QA subsets but states that exact image-level overlap with public benchmarks cannot be fully excluded, leaving open the possibility that part of the reported gain reflects reused content. To address this, run article-level and perceptual near-duplicate audits against every benchmark, publish hashes and exclusions, and report residual overlap and sensitivity results.

The clinical retrieval probe may overstate independence. Images from the same patients are treated as individual samples, and morphology concepts are normalized with an open LLM while only a sample receives resident verification. To address this, use patient-grouped splits and bootstrap procedures, add stratified clinician adjudication, and compare raw, normalized, and alternate label mappings.

Selective filtering obscures the weakest curation stage. Pairs with mismatched panel and generated-subcaption counts are removed even though caption alignment is the least strong component, so the final corpus may hide difficult cases. To address this, report stage-wise attrition by panel count and specialty, publish metrics with and without mismatch filtering, and evaluate abstentions explicitly.

Aggregate reporting limits independent effect-size checks. Confidence intervals are provided, but per-task paired differences, raw variance, and the multiplicity policy across many benchmarks are not exposed clearly enough. To address this, release per-task predictions and differences, bootstrap distributions, exact effect sizes, and a prespecified multiplicity analysis.

Novelty and update claims need tighter boundaries. The integration is useful, but several individual ingredients overlap prior curation and biomedical vision-language work, while the promised update cadence is not yet demonstrated. To address this, cite contemporaneous systems, narrow priority language, and publish a second versioned snapshot with a machine-readable change log.

What would most improve this paper

1. Complete the reproducibility package. Release the exact paper commit, environment lockfiles, source and configuration files, manifests, checkpoints, benchmark evaluators, and a smoke test that runs on public data.

2. Establish data and evaluation independence. Audit article-level and perceptual near-duplicates against every benchmark, publish exclusions and residual overlap, and add patient-grouped clinical resampling with raw-versus-normalized label sensitivity analyses.

3. Quantify curation selection effects. Publish stage attrition, subgroup metrics, calibration, and with/without filtering ablations so readers can see how much quality and coverage are traded at each stage.

4. Make the statistics independently checkable. Release paired predictions, per-task differences, bootstrap distributions, uncertainty details, and the multiplicity policy behind the aggregate comparisons.

5. Tighten positioning and demonstrate updating. Add contemporaneous curation references, narrow priority language, and publish a second corpus snapshot with a machine-readable diff.
