SurGe makes a focused and persuasive case that monocular point maps need local surface-oriented evaluation and optimization. The key next step is auditability: release training/evaluation artifacts, validate the normal metric externally, and report latency and uncertainty tradeoffs.

Paper overview

SurGe studies local surface artifacts in feedforward monocular point-map reconstruction. The paper argues that global point-position metrics can miss orientation errors, foreground/background mixing, and thin-structure failures that matter for downstream 3D use.

The method combines a DINOv2-based point-map model with a Neighborhood Attention Decoder and a point gradient matching loss over depth-normalized 3D finite differences. This makes the model architecture and training objective explicitly attend to neighboring-point geometry rather than treating each predicted 3D point mostly in isolation.

The empirical case is broad: across eight zero-shot monocular geometry benchmarks, SurGe reports the best average rank for global point-map AbsRel and consistently improves local point-map and normal evaluations. The paper also provides public inference code, pretrained weights, a command-line workflow, and a Hugging Face demo, which gives readers a practical entry point for trying the model.

Key strengths

Clear and useful problem targeting. The paper identifies a real gap in monocular point-map evaluation: visually plausible global geometry can still contain locally poor surfaces. This framing is especially relevant for mesh generation, robotics, AR, scene editing, and any workflow where surface orientation matters.

Coherent metric-method alignment. The point-map normal metric, point gradient matching loss, and Neighborhood Attention Decoder all target neighboring-point surface consistency. That alignment makes the paper feel technically integrated rather than a collection of disconnected engineering choices.

Strong empirical breadth. Evaluation across eight zero-shot benchmarks and extensive contemporary baselines gives the central claim meaningful coverage across datasets. The reported improvements in local AbsRel and normal MAE are paired with strong global point-map performance, so the local-surface emphasis does not appear to simply trade away global geometry.

Ablations support the two main components. The decoder and surface-loss experiments isolate the contribution of the Neighborhood Attention Decoder and the point gradient matching objective. This makes the method's main design choices easier to interpret.

Open inference ecosystem improves usability. The public repository, pretrained weights, demo, CLI, and app lower the barrier for qualitative inspection and practical experimentation. That release is a meaningful strength even though full training and benchmark reproduction remain incomplete.

Key concerns and constructive suggestions

Training and benchmark reproducibility are incomplete. The released repository supports inference, but external groups cannot yet regenerate the main benchmark tables or ablation suite from public scripts and configs. To address this, the authors should release benchmark manifests, model adapters, alignment and unprojection code, table-generation scripts, training loops, loss implementations, dataset sampling weights, and exact configs for the decoder and loss ablations.

Some comparisons depend on a conversion pipeline that may affect rankings. For depth baselines, MoGe-2-based alignment and unprojection can influence how competing methods are represented in point-map space. To address this, the paper should add a sensitivity analysis over conversion choices and document the exact conversion pipeline so readers can separate model quality from evaluation-adapter effects.

The proposed normal metric is plausible but externally unvalidated. Point-map normal MAE directly targets the artifacts the paper cares about, but the current evidence mainly shows benchmark alignment and motivating examples. To address this, the authors should correlate the metric with human preference, mesh-quality measures, or downstream task performance, especially on thin structures and object boundaries.

Efficiency claims need a matched-latency view. The Neighborhood Attention Decoder is a reasonable architectural response to local mixing, but it increases inference latency relative to simpler decoder alternatives. To address this, the paper should report latency/accuracy curves for smaller NAD variants, larger ConvStack baselines, and hybrid designs under comparable runtime budgets.

Repeated-run uncertainty is missing. The paper reports strong point estimates, but seed variance and training-data-mix sensitivity are not shown. To address this, the authors should add reduced-scale multi-seed experiments and confidence intervals for the key ablations and representative benchmark results.

What would most improve this paper

1. Release a reproducible evaluation package. Publish benchmark manifests, baseline adapters, alignment and unprojection code, and scripts that regenerate the main benchmark tables. This is the highest-impact step for making the empirical claims externally auditable.

2. Release training and ablation configurations. Provide the training loop, loss code, dataset sampling details, and exact configs for NAD, DPT, ConvStack, ViT, and loss ablations. This would turn the paper's strong empirical story into a reproducible method package.

3. Validate the point-map normal metric externally. Add human preference, mesh-quality, or downstream-task correlation analyses so readers can see whether lower normal MAE tracks practical surface quality beyond the paper's own benchmark framing.

4. Add statistical robustness evidence. Report reduced-scale seed analysis and confidence intervals for key ablations. Even a focused robustness check would clarify which gains are stable and which should be interpreted cautiously.

5. Add matched-latency decoder comparisons. Compare smaller NAD, larger ConvStack, and hybrid variants at similar runtime. This would make the deployment tradeoff clearer for users who care about real-time or resource-constrained monocular geometry.
