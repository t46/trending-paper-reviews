ThoughtTrace: Understanding User Thoughts in Real-World LLM Interactions

Jin, Li, Xie, Fang et al. · May 2026 · arXiv:2605.20087

ThoughtTrace’s strongest contribution is a released, independently checkable dataset that adds private first-person reasons and reactions to real multi-turn LLM conversations, a modality missing from standard chat logs. The paper is much less settled when it turns that corpus into measurements and utility claims, because LLMs are used as labelers, filters, rewriters, and judges without enough human calibration or uncertainty reporting. The most important next step is to convert the release into a validated benchmark: human-calibrated labels and judges, matched utility ablations, uncertainty estimates, and reproducible analysis/training pipelines.

Paper overview

ThoughtTrace targets a real gap in LLM interaction data: ordinary chat logs show what users typed, but not why they typed it or how they privately reacted to a model response. The paper operationalizes those missing signals as self-reported thought traces — reasons for user messages and reactions to assistant responses — collected during real multi-turn conversations.

The released dataset contains 1,058 users, 2,155 conversations, 17,058 turns/messages, and 10,174 thought annotations across 20 language models. The schema also includes demographic metadata, AI-use frequency, task summaries, task expectations, model identity, timestamps, messages, and thoughts. The public JSONL release was locally checked and matches the headline counts.

The paper then analyzes conversation topics, multi-turn relationships, thought distinctiveness, thought inferability, reason/reaction taxonomies, and stage dynamics. It also presents two utility demonstrations: next-message prediction with thought-augmented context and alignment training through thought-guided rewrites. The central interpretive split is clear: the dataset release is concrete and valuable, while the strongest analysis and utility claims remain preliminary because much of the evidence is mediated by LLM labeling, filtering, rewriting, and judging.

Key strengths

The dataset adds a missing user-state modality to real LLM conversations. ThoughtTrace contributes private first-person reasons and reactions tied to actual multi-turn chatbot use, rather than only observable message text. That makes the corpus useful for studying intent, satisfaction, user simulation, personalization, and interaction dynamics that standard transcript datasets cannot directly expose.

The collection protocol preserves the distinction between hidden thoughts and assistant-visible dialogue. The paper separates internal annotations from model inputs, so the thought traces are not merely extra prompt text. This design supports the paper’s core claim that the annotation channel captures information adjacent to, but not identical with, the conversation transcript.

The release is inspectable and large enough for descriptive research. A corpus with more than one thousand participants, more than two thousand conversations, and more than ten thousand thought annotations is substantial for a first-person thought dataset. The repository and Hugging Face dataset make the artifact available, and the released counts can be independently verified.

The schema is practically useful for downstream researchers. The combination of thoughts, messages, task information, model identity, timestamps, and user metadata gives researchers multiple ways to filter, stratify, and test hypotheses. The format is simple enough for external users to inspect directly rather than depending on a complex bespoke tooling stack.

The utility demonstrations make the artifact more than a descriptive release. The next-message prediction and DPO-style alignment experiments show plausible ways that thought annotations can become training or evaluation signals. Even though the current evidence needs stronger validation, the demonstrations give future work a concrete starting point.

Key concerns and constructive suggestions

LLM-mediated measurement carries too much of the paper’s evidentiary load. LLMs label topics and relations, score semantic coverage, infer thoughts, filter high-quality annotations, rewrite training examples, and judge downstream outputs. Prompt disclosure is helpful, but it does not establish that these measurements track human interpretations of user thought. To address this, the authors should human-label stratified samples for topic labels, relation labels, reason/reaction categories, coverage judgments, and next-message comparisons, then report inter-annotator agreement and LLM-vs-human calibration.

The main quantitative claims lack uncertainty reporting. The paper reports aggregate means and win rates without per-example distributions, confidence intervals, significance tests, seed variance, or paired benchmark uncertainty. This makes it hard to tell which differences are stable and which are artifacts of sampling, judge variance, or training noise. To address this, the authors should add bootstrap intervals for prediction results, seed variance for DPO training, and paired uncertainty estimates for Arena-Hard-style evaluations.

The utility experiments do not cleanly isolate the value of thought content. The next-message experiment is computed on a high-quality LLM-filtered subset, while the alignment comparison changes both signal type and supervision density: the thought-guided condition uses more examples than the message-guided ThoughtTrace condition. The DPO setup also includes GPT-5.4-generated rewrites, so the result reflects a pipeline rather than raw thoughts alone. To address this, the authors should run unfiltered, high-quality-only, and random-filter controls; match training instance counts across message-guided and thought-guided conditions; and ablate reason-only, reaction-only, label-only, free-text-only, and full-thought variants.

The public repository verifies the dataset but not the strongest paper results. The release includes the JSONL data, examples, schema documentation, and basic notebooks, but not runnable scripts for the paper’s analyses, LLM labeling, next-message prediction, rewrite generation, DPO training, or evaluation. This limits independent reproduction of the claims that most need scrutiny. To address this, the authors should release scripts for all tables and figures, prompt files, cached intermediate labels where licensing permits, exact model/API versions, DPO data construction code, evaluation configs, and a reproducible environment file.

The novelty framing needs to stay narrow and precise. Prior work already includes real-world chat corpora, preference/feedback datasets such as PRISM, latent-state user modeling, and explicit think-aloud search-simulation data. These do not erase ThoughtTrace’s contribution, but they do weaken broad claims about first user-thought or user-state data. To address this, the authors should consistently frame the contribution as a large-scale real-world multi-turn LLM conversation dataset with private first-person turn-level reason and reaction annotations.

What would most improve this paper

1. Add human validation for the measurement pipeline. A stratified human-labeled validation set would directly address the paper’s largest evidentiary risk. The most useful version would cover descriptive labels, coverage judgments, thought inferability, next-message comparisons, and LLM-judge calibration.

2. Report uncertainty for the central result tables. Per-example distributions, bootstrap confidence intervals, paired tests, and seed variance would make the empirical claims much easier to interpret. This is especially important for the alignment comparison, where the most relevant differences are modest and currently reported without run-level reliability evidence.

3. Fix the utility ablations so the thought signal is isolated. The paper should compare unfiltered thoughts, high-quality-filtered thoughts, random-filter controls, matched-size message-guided and thought-guided DPO sets, and separate reason/reaction/label/free-text variants. That would distinguish the value of private thought content from filtering, supervision density, and rewrite-pipeline effects.

4. Release the full analysis and training pipeline. The current artifact is a good dataset release but a weak reproducibility release for the experiments. Scripts, prompts, cached derived labels, model versions, DPO construction code, evaluation configs, and an environment lockfile would let others audit and extend the results.

5. Tighten the claim language and build toward a benchmark. The strongest phrasing is not that ThoughtTrace reveals true internal cognition, but that it captures valuable self-reported reasons and reactions missing from transcript-only corpora. A natural next step is a benchmark testing whether thought-trained simulators predict held-out human behavior better than message-only simulators.
